MedRepublic

Client MedRepublic
Role UX Designer
Platform Responsive Web
Year 2017

Medical tourism connects patients with life-changing procedures abroad — but MedRepublic's site made a complex decision feel even harder to make.

Medical tourism is the practice of traveling to another country to receive medical, dental, or surgical care that may be equal or better in quality — and significantly more affordable. MedRepublic helps patients from the US and Canada find those options.

My team was tasked with a website redesign with two clear goals: be more transparent to users, and simplify the information architecture — all within a two-week deadline.

2 wks Project timeline
3 High-traffic pages redesigned
GA Data-driven with Google Analytics access
Process

Start with the data.

My team and I worked together to identify the areas of the site with the most traffic and usage. MedRepublic granted us access to their Google Analytics, allowing us to trace user flows and understand how visitors were actually navigating the site.

Three pages emerged as the highest-traffic destinations: the Home Page, Search Results, and Doctor Profile. We focused our redesign efforts entirely on these three.

Page 01 — Home

Give users a
clear starting point.

The Home Page was the primary entry point to the MedRepublic experience. I focused attention on two elements: the search bar and the 5 Steps to MedRepublic.

The search bar was the main gateway to the rest of the site, so I wanted it prominent and impossible to miss. I also recommended surfacing popular searches beneath it — giving first-time visitors immediate direction and ideas.

The 5 Steps section educated users on how MedRepublic worked. I pushed to bring it above the fold — sitting directly below the search bar — so new visitors could quickly understand the process without needing to scroll.

Page 02 — Search Results

Hierarchy driven
by user testing.

The Search Results page displayed a grid of doctor cards with quick-glance information. But what information should rank highest? My team ran user tests to find out what mattered most when booking a doctor online.

Through testing, we found that education ranked first, followed by price savings, then doctor reviews. We applied those priorities directly to the card hierarchy.

Page 03 — Doctor Profile

One page,
all the information.

The Doctor Profile page was information-dense by necessity. We ran a second round of user tests to determine the best way to group content and reduce unnecessary navigation.

The solution: consolidate Doctor Bio, Patient Reviews, and Procedure Details into a single tabbed nav bar, eliminating excessive scrolling and putting all the critical information in one place. We also introduced a sub-nav for categories with additional sub-sections.

Takeaway

Simplicity as
a trust signal.

MedRepublic offers a genuinely compelling value proposition to users facing high-cost medical decisions. A site that's transparent, easy to navigate, and surfaces the right information at the right moment isn't just a better UX — it's a trust signal for people making some of the most important decisions of their lives.

Reflection

By simplifying pages and surfacing information more efficiently, we aimed to increase booked appointments and help users feel more confident about participating in medical tourism. With more time, my team and I would have run additional rounds of user testing to further optimize for conversion — but even within two weeks, data-led decisions made a meaningful difference.
